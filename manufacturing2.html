Etching:

1. Oxide Formation A layer of oxide is implanted on the wafer. This is most often done by exposing the wafer to steam at very high temperatures.
2. Application of Photoresist A layer of organic photoresist is applied. This is like the film in a camera.
3. Masking and Exposure to Ultraviolet Radiation A mask is applied and UV light is shone through the gaps. UV light is used because of its shorter wavelength; a shorter wavelength means that it can pass through a smaller mask. The UV light hardens (fixes) the photoresist.
4. Removal of Unexposed Photoresist The unhardened mask is removed with an organic solvent.
5. Etching in Hydroflouric Acid Hydroflouric (HF) acid is used to etch away the Silicon Oxide. Since HF is inorganic, it does not attack the photoresist.
6. Removal of Exposed Photoresist The remaining photoresist is washed away. The wafer is now ready for doping with another type of silicon, or for adding contacts.

Making the Transistor:

(A) A p-type wafer (silicon doped with Boron) has an epilayer of n-type (silicon doped with Phosphorous or Arsenic).
(B) A mask is used to implant Silicon Dioxide for the insulator.
(C) Acceptor atoms (Boron) are diffused into the window in the Silicon Dioxide.
(D) Using another mask, additional Silicon Dioxide is grown. Donor atoms (elements like Arsenic with excess electrons) are implanted.
(E) Finally, yet another mask is used to grow even more Silicon Dioxide. Once again, a mask is used to implant evaporated Aluminum or Copper for the contacts. This is a Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT).

These transistors are what are used in microprocessors and memory. They take less power then BJTs but are made in the exact same way.

All this work is done in 'clean rooms', as even the smallest particle of dust can ruin a whole chip! The workers wear 'bunny suits' to keep dust and skin particales from getting into the air.

These days much of the process is automated, so the technicians have to monitor many sophisticated machines.

A fabrication lab (fab) requires large amounts of infrastructure to deal with all the chemicals, high temperatures, and pressures used inside.
